# GRE Resources

I had to take the GRE in the Fall of 2025. I had vowed to never take another standardized exam after suffering through the GED, the SAT, the SAT subject tests, and the TOEFL a decade ago, but I ended up wanting to work with an advisor at a school that required the GRE for funding. This repository contains some of resources I used and created to help me study for it.

## Verbal

There's a book in here but I didn't end up using it. My tip is just to read a lot of academic writing or long-form journalism about things that interest you. I think it helps to be aware of the the differences among entailment, presupposition, and implicature (see `entailment-presupposition-implicature.pdf`).

As for vocabulary: I created a spreadsheet (`gre-vocab.csv`) with words that were the most difficult to me from the GregMat vocab spreadsheet, and filled it out with etymologies and other helpful bits of information to aid retrieval.

Key of abbreviations:

- Lt - Latin
- Gk - Greek
- OE - Old English
- ME - Middle English
- En - English
- OF - Old French
- Fr - French
- Gr - German
- It - Italian
- cf - Lt. confer "compare"
- sth - something
- qqc - Fr. quelque chose "something"

## Quant

There are a few books in here but I didn't end up using them either. GregMat PrepSwift is affordable and has comprehesive coverage of the material.

I feel that GRE quant doesn't cover anything super advanced, but tests problem solving under timed pressure. My tips:

1. Master the basics. The GRE tests your problem solving skills **presupposing** that you thoroughly and fluently know the material. If I hadn't brushed up on topics in arithmetic, for example, I would have been in trouble.
2. Be able to prove small things to yourself. It doesn't have to be a rigorous proof, but you should be able to sanity-check, using a simplified example, things like the sum of an arithmetic sequence, or the relationship between the central and inscribed angles in a circle.
3. Skip through questions (but come back later). Spend the first 30 seconds running your eyes over every problem, then start with the easiest problems. That way, you'll have plenty of time to work on the hard problems, without worrying about having enough time at the end to solve the easy ones.

## Analytical Writing

I struggled with this section a lot. I spent a third of the time coming up with miserably contrived examples for the miserably contrived prompt ("When one is making a decision, it is better to have a limited number of options"). Tips:

1. I've read that you have to stick to a formula (introduction, 3 supporting examples or 2 supporting and 1 counter, then conclusion) and that you have to aim for 500–600 words. As someone who makes brevity a priority in writing, I wrote about 400 words, and accordingly didn't do well.
2. They don't allow CTRL + arrow keys to jump between words! This adds to a lot of wasted time.
